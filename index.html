- With zero formal training, relying on my intuition and various random pieces of advice I picked up from people smarter than me - I've more or less successfully lead my teams to their goals. - On that path, I’ve learned tremendously, improved myself and hopefully, my teams. - I've would like to share today with you some advices I wish someone told me before embarking down this path.

The Peter Principle

- The Peter Principle states that people in a structure tend to rise through promotions until they reach their level of incompetence. - So in other words, every time you get a promotion, you are in a way are on the edge of being capable of doing your job. - Uptil now, you were an expert in your field, and now - You will probably wonder "Am I ready for this?" - And maybe you are, maybe you are not, - but if someone thinks you can do it and if you are conscious enough that you realize the weight of the decision:
- Do it! - You will never feel 100% certain that you'll be able to handle this. - If you did, it wouldn't be a on the edge, would it? - Being a lead in any way or form is not a new upgraded version of the job you've had before. - It's almost entirely new job. - You are still within the same bussiness domain, but outside of your safe zone.
- You have to take care of new roles that you are not prepared for. - First one is a familiar one, but now it's got a twist.

Developer

- I'll start this one with a story. - When working with one team I struck a deal with management to be a Lead developer and lead a small team of about 6 devs. - Original plan was 20% of my time I'll do team management work, while the other 80% dev. - It kind of sounded dreamy for me. - First month and a half: 4 lines of code. - Don't get me wrong. I'm not saying it's a bad thing. - But be aware what you are getting yourself into. - The bigger the team, the bigger the project, less time you're going to spend actually writting code. - You need to realise:
- You are not a code monkey anymore.
- You are the architect, the decision maker, the organizer and more than anything else, the reviewer. - You are placed in this position to produce more code than ever, just not by yourself. - So if you are not creating this code, who is? - This of course leads us to hiring.

Hiring

- For the first interview in which I sat as the interviewer I explicitly asked to be included. - What's the worst that can happen, right? - Soon, my wish was granted.
- I don't know who sweated more. - The developer looking for a job or me. - When I'm nervous I drink a lot of water. - So at the beggining of the interview my collegue asked the candidate would they like something to drink. - I just wanted to shout: "A bucket of water for me, please!" - I was worrying not to look like a total tool in front of the candidate. - Probably spent more time thinking how clever do I sound instead of how smart is the candidate. - In my head it was a mess.
- But after a while (few interviews unfortunately) I realized: - I don't need to sound like the smartest person in the room. - The candidate does, so why not help them with that. - I became the good cop. - Nodding along with their explanations, saying stupid jokes or even just going "me too" to their stories. - I tried to make this formal talk as pleasant and informal as I could. - By reducing the pressure I got a more honest face of the candidate and their way of thinking. - I have one golden rule that I established few years ago and still hold as the most important one:
- Don't be an ass. - Tech can be taught, but I can't teach you what kind of person you should be. - I'm not very good at reading people. - When it comes to some human interactions sometimes I'm like a deer in head lights.
- Interviewing for me, but I believe for most of the people, just doesn’t come naturally. - It’s a skill that improves with practice. - But this is trial and error in production.
- Because it is, - I spend a lot of time preparing for the interviews. - I have notes with questions, with possible pathways that can lead to other questions. - All that to have smooth back-and-forth flow of conversation. - You assume that the candidate took some time to prepare, so it's only polite to do the same. - And so you hire someone, they are great, and then they are not.

Termination

- This is the other side of the coin. - A lot of people are not talking about it. - And it's probably the hardest thing to do. - Luckily, I only had to do it once. - I don't even know if there is a way to do it gently. - This should be a last resort solution. - Make sure that you've exhausted all options before this. - Give your team members regular reviews, if necessary give them negative reviews. - Maybe they are not aware of their actions and have a strong desire to change. - Just make sure you fire a few warning shots in the weeks and months before.
- After you've pulled the trigger, - Explain to the folks still on the team why it came to this. - Don't wait too long to do it, this is a big thing. - Such info needs to come from you. - If you wait too long it will come from the other side of that dialogue and you don't want that.

Delegation

- You hired some people, hopefully fired none, got some assigned and you have your team. - When I first started being a lead I did one thing extremly wrong. - I wanted to work on the fun, complex and new stuff myself. - It came from a place where I was selfish - but also not trustworthy enough. - Of course, that backfired. - And I became the bottleneck of the project. - I had people in my team who maybe wanted to combat this issues themself - I didn't given them a chance. - Because I was too preoccupied with my work and my problems. - The ones I myself delegated to me.
- Listen to your teammates, ask them on what do they want to work on, accomodate them if you can, if not, give a clear explanation why. - So get to know each team member and their streghts and weekneses and give out tasks accordingly. - But work on your 6th sense and move people into new directions.
- Try to read you team's inner dinamics. - See who works best with whom. - Fortify existing relationships, encourage new ones. - That can only benefit them and the company as a whole.
- In extreme situations - you're the one who's only task is triage! - No time for discussions, you need to know on a spot who can and will deliver the solution.
- Don't ping them non-stop, don't throw new logs to the trash fire that is happening. - Let them do what they do best: - Solve problems.
- For me, either everyday delegation or delegation in emergency creates a really weird feeling. - I'm in control, but at the same time I control nothing. - I'm responsible, but I don't directly do anything tangible. - Success of my job is solely based on the success of my teammates. - That can be scary. - I sometimes find myself thinking:

People

- "People are the worst" - Which is a horrible notion if you manage people. - But sometimes, it happens. - For instance, - you have a breach in your production database, a hacker demanding ransom, and the dev working on it decided that it's time for his haircut and just leaves. - It's hard to love the whole human kind at that moment. - But with a good team, a team you trust, another thought comes to mind:

People

are

the best

- "People are the best" - People love to shine, people love being heros, people want to do work they can be proud of. - Be the Alfred to your Batmans. - Be Jarvis to your Iron Mans. - Clear the road in front of them, and let them work. - Open the doors for you team and - Trust you team.

Communication

- And trust is in large part built through communication.
- Have regular 1-on-1s - you can have them scheduled or not. - in my experience, scheduled works better, - it leaves time for team member to prepare. - If you don't schedule it, they will probably not happen. - Just don't do it too often or keep it running for too long. - And if you want to earn your team members trust, be prepared to open yourself too. - These 1-on-1s work both ways. - Be honest there. - Prepare to be vulnerable. - Let you guard down and show your true face. - After all, that is what you are hoping to get from your team too.
- With group meetings, - Learn how to lead and moderate them. - Know who should be in a meeting and who gains nothing from it. - Some time ago, I was just a regular dev in that team, - I was in a conference call with my wireless headphones on sitting comfortably in my home office. - It was a meeting that took a wrong turn somewhere and now there were 10 of us trapped listening to conversation between just 2 people. - First thing I did was subtly turning off the camera and went to make a fresh cup of tea. - Then... I started folding laundry. - Then I put a new load in the washing machine. - Then I did the dishes. - I also cleaned out some leftovers from the fridge. - Was just starting to look for the vacuum when people started to say goodbye. - My general rule of thumb: - if you are talking in a meeting for longer than 3 minutes about a topic that only concers a small part of people in the meeting, that should be a separate meeting probably. - Capture meeting decisions and share them afterwards as a TLDR; doc. - You are creating a paper trail of decisions and action items - and giving chance for others in team or even company to see the updates easily.
- I have a nice bonus suggestion for you I've picked up from a client: - start a group meeting with a random question, but totally random. - It can span from - "which animal describes you today?" - to... - "what's the weirdest thing in your fridge?" - It's an ice breaker, somebody will always say something interesting or funny, and all of the sudden you are all talking and sharing stuff around the same topic. - See, out-of-box cohesion!

Praise

- As a good leader you need to recognize and magnify the accomplishments of your team mates. - Praise and commendations are a powerful tool that you can use to boost team mood and engagement.
- Celebrate progress. - That doesn't mean you should handle out silly participations trophys, - Use the power of compliment only when there is something tangible to compliment. - Don't force it. - It will start sounding insincere.
- But also don't focus on only the big accomplishments. - People are everyday working on problems that are not on the forefront of you product, - but are still important. - Small wins that move the product forward are the thing you are looking for here.

Closing

- There are many more roles that I haven't touched today. - But this is a 25 minutes talk so - I would like for you to take home if nothing else than my final pieces of advices:

Learn

- As with you previous dev role, you need to continue learning new skills. - If you got it this far, you were probably good at that, so use those old learning skills to pick up this hot potato.

Trust

- The same way you need to trust people, people need to trust you. - This takes time. - It won't happen over night.

Protect

- Often you are the one that's protecting the team from "higher forces" and if you are doing a good job of making your team feel safe, you will earn team's trust. - So continue asking those questions people don't dare to ask.

Open discussions

- Don't avoid hard conversations and decisions. - Be open about them with the team. - If you can, democratize decision making. - Make everybody feel included. - Encourage discussion.
- But know when to end.
