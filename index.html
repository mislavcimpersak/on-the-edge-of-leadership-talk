Edge of Incompetence


==


The Peter Principle

- Going to make some segway into roles. - For each role probably going to tell a short story. - First one being developer.

Developer

- When working with one team I struck a deal with management to be a Lead developer and lead a small team of about 6 devs. - Original plan was 20% of my time I'll do team management work, while the other 80% dev. - It kind of sounded dreamy for me. - First month and a half: 4 lines of code. - It was 8 am, a bug report came in, the rest of my team was still asleep and I was soooo happy to actually write something useful and tangible for a change. - Be aware what you are getting your self in to. - The bigger the team, the bigger the project, less time you're going to spend actually writting code. - You need to realise:
- You are not a code monkey anymore.
- You are the architect, the decision maker, the organizer and more than anything else, the reviewer. - You are placed in this position to produce more code than ever, just not by yourself. - So if you are not creating this code, who is? This of course leads us to hiring.

Hiring

- For the first interview in which I sat as the interviewer I explicitly asked to be included. - Soon, my wish was granted.
- I don't know who sweated more. - The developer looking for a job or me. - I was worrying not to look like a total tool in front of the candidate. - Probably spent more time thinking how clever do I sound instead of how smart is the candidate. - In my head it was a mess.
- But after a while (few interviews unfortunately) I realized: I don't need to sound like the smartest person in the room. - The candidate does, so why not help them with that. - I became the good cop. - Nodding along with their explanations, saying stupid jokes or even just going "me too" to their stories. - I tried to make this formal talk as pleasant and informal as I could. - By reducing the pressure I got a more honest face of the candidate and his way of thinking.
- I have one golden rule that I established few years ago and still hold as the most important one: - Tech can be tough, but I can't teach you not to be ass. - (picture of a donkey)
- I'm not very good at reading people. - Interviewing for me, but I believe for most of the people, just doesn’t come naturally. - It’s a skill that improves with practice. - I spend a lot of time preparing for the interviews. - I have notes with questions, with possible pathways that can lead to other questions. - All that to have smooth back-and-forth flow of conversation. - You assume that the candidate took some time to prepare, so it's only polite to do the same. - And so you hire someone, they are great, and then they are not.

Termination

- This is the other side of the coin. - A lot of people are not talking about it. - And that's probably it's the hardest thing to do. - Luckily, I only had to do it once. - I don't even know if there is a way to do it gently. - This should be a last resort solution. - Make sure that you've exhausted all options before this. - Give your team members regular reviews, if necessary give them negative reviews. - After you've pulled the trigger, - Ask yourself, was there something I could have done to prevent this? - And explain to the folks still on the team why it came to this. - Don't wait too long to do it, this is a big thing. - Such info needs to come from you. - If you wait too long it will come from the other side of that dialogue and you don't want that.
- Paranoid bonus tip: have a quick way to rotate your security keys. Just in case.

Delegation/Team

- You are one, but there are many people in your team. - Listen to your teammates, ask them on what do they want to work on, accomodate them if you can, if not, give a clear explanation why. - Devs are often quiet; - So get to know each team member and their streghts and weekneses and give out tasks accordingly. - But work on your 6th sense and move people into new directions.
- Try to read you team's inner dinamics. - See who works best with whom. - Fortify existing relationships, encourage new ones. - That can only benefit them and the company as a whole.
- In extreme situations - you're the one who's only task is triage! - No time for discussions, you need to know on a spot who can and will deliver the solution.
- Don't ping them non-stop, don't throw new logs to the trash fire that is happening. - Let them do what they do best: - Solve problems.
- For me, either everyday delegation or delegation in emergency creates a really weird feeling. - I'm in control, but at the same time I control nothing. - I'm responsible, but I don't directly do anything tangible. - Success of my job is solely based on the success of my teammates. - That can be scary.

(superhero image) - Just clear the space in front of them, and let them work. (open road image) - Just: - Trust you team.

Communication

- And that leads us to communication. - For which: - you are always available. - team is not, and that's ok.
- Have regular 1-on-1s - you can have them scheduled or not. - in my experience, scheduled works better, - it leaves time for team member to prepare. - Just don't do it too often of keep it running for too long. - And if you want to earn your team members trust, be prepared to open your self. - These 1-on-1s work both ways. - Be honest.
- With group meetings, - Learn how to lead and take control of them. - Capture meeting decisions and share them afterwards as a TLDR; doc.
- I have a nice bonus suggestion for you I've picked up from a client: - start a group meeting with a random question, but totally random. - it can span from - which person do you admire? - can you tell some random piece of information that you recently learned that surprised you? - to... - what's the weirdest thing in your fridge? - it's an ice breaker, somebody will always say something funny, and all of the sudden you are all talking and sharing stuff around the same topic. - See, out-of-box cohesion!

Closing

Learn

- As with you previous dev role, you need to continue learning new skills. - If you got it this far, you were probably good at that, so use those old learning skills to pick up this hot potato.

Trust

- The same way you need to trust people, people need to trust you.

Protect

- Often you are the one that's protecting the team from "higher forces" and if you are doing a good job of making your team feel safe, you will earn team's trust. - So continue asking those questions people don't dare to ask.

Set to win

- Prepare the ground for you team. - Give them everything they need to succeed.

Open discussions

- Don't avoid hard conversations and decisions. - Be open about them with the team. - If you can, democratize decision making. - Make everybody feel included. - Encourage discussion.

- But know when to end.
